The thread. I’ve spent 15 years replacing manual content production with systems — programmatic landing pages, templated long-tail content, SEO middle platforms, and now GEO/AEO for AI assistants. Three case studies below illustrate the pattern at three scales: a modular per-word system that took a dictionary to 200K DAU, a 1.5M-page system that grew an OTA’s non-brand organic orders ~9× over four years, and a global SEO middle platform that found 99.97% of indexed pages didn’t earn their existence — a single delete-button press moved the long-tail metric by 7×.

Case 1 — Programmatic SEO at 1.5M-page scale (eLong / Expedia, 2012–2017)

Problem

eLong (one of China’s largest OTAs at the time) had a long-tail traffic problem. Travelers don’t just search for hotels by city — they search for hotels near specific places: airports, train stations, hospitals, tourist sites, conference centers, individual streets and metro stops. Each of those queries deserved its own landing page, but there were millions of them, and manually authoring even a fraction was not viable. The previous external SEO vendor had tried 10M+ pure-spam keyword pages — Baidu de-indexed almost all of them within a month. The lesson: long-tail SEO needs business logic, not just keywords.

Approach

  • One templated page form for every POI type. Same layout, same content blocks, same data sources — not separate archetypes for airport / hospital / attraction / transit hub. The page was a single traveler-decision-journey view: how to get there from each transport hub, local services within 5km, ≥3 bookable hotels nearby with an interactive map. One form, all POI types.
  • Programmatic generation. Cron job pulled POI data from Gaode / Baidu APIs (with map-area services: banks, transit, attractions, hospitals) + eLong’s own hotel inventory. Rendered static HTML + CSS + JS with an embedded interactive map component. CDN-cacheable. Same pages for PC and mobile (responsive). A separate cron updated the sitemap; Baidu discovered new pages through it.
  • Quality gate, not search volume. The ship-or-not condition was ≥3 bookable hotels within 5km — a real business-value condition. Map providers returned 15–20M raw POIs; after dedup and filtering, 1.5M made the cut. ~90% were eventually indexed (~1.35M). I did not filter by search volume: real landmarks eventually get searched.
  • Internal linking architecture. Built as opportunities arose, not on a strict schedule: city pages → key POIs in that city; business-district pages (a special POI type) → nearby key POIs; hotel detail pages → nearby POIs. Hub-and-spoke from inventory to long tail.
  • Discipline, not feature count. Built a 150K-UV/day intracity transit-routes feature, then closed it directly: eLong earned on hotels only, and this had zero business value. No escalation needed — the standing principle from the CEO was “the board measures me on orders only, don’t build traffic that has no value.”

Results

  • ~9× growth in non-brand organic orders over 4 years (2×, 2×, 1.5×, 1.5× year-on-year)
  • 20–30M RMB annual net profit in the best year (conservative estimate using company-wide cost basis — real number is likely higher)
  • Total SEO channel reached ~10% of all orders by end of tenure (mostly brand-SEO; the non-brand slice from this work is a fraction, tracked separately)
  • Team evolved from 1 SEO operator + 5 editors to 5 engineers, 0 SEO operators, 0 editors — manual content production was the wrong shape for the page volume needed

Adjacent: SEM automation

Same engineering mindset applied to paid: replaced a 15-person manual SEM operation with a Ruby on Rails platform and a unified search-engine API abstraction. Steady-state 20M keywords across Baidu, 360, Sogou, Soso (now defunct), and Shenma (mobile-only). Hive-based attribution joined spend to order value at the keyword level, making spend-vs-profit visible by default. ROI +50% vs. the manual baseline.

Case 2 — Modular per-word pages (Youdao Dictionary, 2011–2012)

Problem

Youdao Dictionary was a translation/lookup product fighting for organic visibility against larger competitors. The search opportunity wasn’t in transactional queries — it was in the informational long tail: people looking up terms from their own field of work or study, in context. Each term deserved a page that answered the real question behind the search, not just a dictionary entry.

Approach

  • Per-word pages, foreign ↔ Chinese. Built each page around a single word. For a foreign word (e.g. notebook) the page gave the Chinese translation (笔记本) plus everything a user should know about that word — definition, explanation, sample sentences, related content. Not a bare A→B translation; a full answer to what the searcher actually wanted to know.
  • Each module at its own URL. The content on a word’s page was split into standalone module pages — sample sentences, definition, and so on — each with its own URL. A search for “sample sentences of notebook” or “what does notebook mean” landed directly on the right module, not a generic entry. The wiki lived in the structure of these independently discoverable module pages.
  • Term corpus from upstream of search. One templated pattern — not per-domain archetypes. The term list came from the Sogou input-method’s Cell of Words (词库): the input method sits upstream of search, so it captures what people actually type before they ever reach a search engine. That corpus spans every domain naturally, which is where the domain coverage came from.
  • Discovery through a layered sitemap. Homepage → sub-homepage → each domain → subcategory with pagination, so search engines could crawl the full tree. Each main translation page linked out to its module subpages.

Results

  • Dictionary DAU 10K → 180–200K
  • Translation-product brand traffic +100%
  • Awarded Best Employee 4 months after joining

Note from 2026. This pattern — modular, intent-shaped per-word pages, each module independently discoverable — is what modern developer-documentation SEO has rediscovered. Mintlify/Docusaurus/ReadMe sites that rank well today are doing structurally what we did at Youdao 15 years ago. The platforms are different; the principles are identical.

Case 3 — Global SEO middle platform (Alibaba, 2017–2019)

Problem

AliExpress and Alibaba.com had been losing organic traffic for years, despite having two of the largest e-commerce catalogs in the world. Past long-tail SEO work had been adding pages. That was the wrong direction.

Investigation

  • Both sites had 5–6 billion indexed pages each. AliExpress measures success by orders; Alibaba.com is B2B and measures success by quote requests.
  • Counting the pages that actually drove business outcomes (orders on AliExpress, quote requests on Alibaba.com), only about 2 million earned their existence — and a strict count would put it closer to 500K.
  • That’s roughly 0.03%–0.04% of indexed pages. The remaining 99.97% were diluting crawl budget and drowning out the good content.
  • Diagnosis: content convergence problem, not a content generation problem.

What I did

  • Phase 1 — content convergence. AliExpress had separate buyer-side and seller-side product detail pages, both indexed. Removed the redundant set. Long-tail organic traffic went up ~70K UV/day almost immediately — a 7× lift on the metric that mattered, with no new content. Sometimes the answer to “more traffic” is “fewer pages.”
  • Phase 2 — the SEO middle platform. Built shared SEO infrastructure for AliExpress, Alibaba.com, and Taobao Overseas — hreflang, canonicalization, schema, sitemaps, internal linking, all at platform scale. The principle generalized: stop building parallel per-market systems; build one platform that does it right and every site benefits.

Results

  • Reversed multi-year organic-traffic decline
  • Long-tail non-brand organic on AliExpress / Alibaba.com: < 1,000 UV/day → ~200,000 UV/day
  • Taobao Overseas long-tail: near-zero → added ~150K UV/day
  • Full-funnel UX optimization lifted organic conversion rate by 10% on top of the traffic gains
  • Saved 90%+ headcount vs. the per-market, per-team model that would have been required otherwise

Current: GEO / AEO on Doubao and other AI assistants (2025—)

The same engineering mindset extends naturally to the next surface. At Cheetah Mobile (2025–26) I ran a production GEO operation on Chinese AI assistants (Doubao and others) — the full daily loop from query investigation through content to measurement, across three China-market B2B lines. Attribution was closed through a customer-service intake that verified where each client first heard the brand, surfacing AI-assistant discovery. GEO’s contribution to orders grew from 10% to ~50% on average (a 63% peak for two weeks) in under a year. Now extending the same approach for a leading automotive platform.

The patterns from cases 1–3 transfer directly: intent → archetype mapping, entity content with topical authority, platform-level automation, content convergence. The surfaces change; the engineering mindset doesn’t.
